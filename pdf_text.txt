Chapter 1 Introduction 
1.1 Rationale of the Study 
Global food security is facing an unprecedented convergence of threats driven by the 
rapid degradation of land and water resources, the intensifying volatility of climate 
change, and a critical demographic crisis within the agricultural workforce. Reports from 
the FAO indicate that human -induced land degradation now affects vast areas of the 
globe, pushing productive ecosystems to a breaki ng point precisely when agricultural 
output must increase by 50% to feed a growing population  [1] [2]. This environmental 
strain is compounded by a severe labor shortage, particularly in agrarian nations like the 
Philippines, where the average age of farmers has risen significantly. Studies warn that 
this aging demographic creates a "dead -end path" for food security, as the younger 
generation increasingly rejects farming due to its physical hardships  and low economic 
returns, leaving a vacuum that manual labor can no longer fill  [3] [4]. Consequently, the 
imperative for automation is no longer just about efficiency; it is a fundamental necessity 
for survival to decouple food production from the dwindling availability of human labor. 
However, the current landscape of agricultural robotics and automation remains 
fragmented and often ill -suited for sustainable, resource -efficient cultivation. While  
Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs) have been proposed as mobile solutions, they suffer 
from significant mechanical complexity and navigational challenges in unstructured 
environments [5]. More critically, the physical interaction  between mobile robots and the 
soil introduces a detrimental factor known as soil compaction, which degrades land fertility 
and restricts root growth. Research on "Controlled Traffic Farming" has established that 
separating machinery traffic from plant roo t zones is essential for soil preservation, with 
7 | P a g e  
 
studies confirming that preventing machinery from traversing the growing area can 
increase crop yields by 20 –35% due to natural soil decompression [6]. Furthermore, 
UGVs often st ruggle with traction and stability in muddy or uneven terrain, leading to 
operational failures that small-scale farmers cannot afford [7]. 
In parallel to the mechanical limitations of mobile robots, existing stationary automati on 
systems frequently rely on "dumb" logic that fails to adapt to dynamic environmental 
conditions. Many current automated systems utilize simple Real -Time Clock (RTC) 
modules to trigger irrigation on rigid schedules, such as watering every day at specific  
times regardless of the actual weather [8]. This lack of intelligence leads to severe 
resource inefficiency, such as watering crops shortly before a rainstorm or failing to 
irrigate during unexpected heatwaves. Such rigid sche duling not only wastes scarce 
freshwater but can also cause physiological stress to plants through waterlogging, which 
depletes oxygen in the root zone and leads to cell death [9]. Furthermore, conventional 
systems often lack integrated nutrient management, leading to the indiscriminate "blanket 
application" of fertilizers. This practice creates nutrient imbalances that degrade soil 
health over time and contribute to environmental pollution through nitrate leaching . 
Agri – 3D addresses these critical architectural and logical deficiencies by implementing 
a unified, fixed -frame Cartesian (XYZ) Gantry system. Unlike mobile robots, the gantry 
traverses above the cultivation bed on fixe d rails, ensuring zero soil compaction and 
providing the high mechanical stability required for precise tool positioning [10].This 
architecture is superior to articulated arms or polar coordinate systems, which suffer from 
non-linear kinematic errors and scalability issues that make them unsuitable for 
rectangular growing plots [11] [12].By utilizing a stable gantry framework, Agri – 3D can 
8 | P a g e  
 
deploy a multi -function tool he ad capable of performing irrigation, fertilization, and 
weeding with millimeter -level precision, a capability that is essential for high -density 
planting where every centimeter of space is valuable [13]. 
To move beyond the limi tations of simple automation toward true autonomy, Agri – 3D 
integrates a weather -adaptive control system that fuses real -time soil sensor data with 
local weather API forecasting. Instead of reacting blindly to dry soil sensors, the system 
evaluates the probability of rainfall to inhibit irrigation if precipitation is imminent, thereby 
conserving water and preventing the detrimental effects of over -saturation [14]. This 
predictive capability is complemented by an AI -driven fertigation subsystem that utilizes 
machine learning algorithms to analyze real -time soil nutrient data (NPK). By predicting 
the exact fertilizer dosage required for specific crop stages, the system optimizes plant 
growth while minimizing chemical runoff, offer ing a sustainable alternative to manual 
fertilization [15]. Finally, the integration of computer vision allows for mechanical weeding, 
where the robot identifies and removes invasive plants without the need for harmful 
chemical herbicides, ensuring a holistic and environmentally responsible approach to 
modern farming [16]. 
1.2 Statement of the Problem 
Ensuring global food security is increasingly challenged by land degradation, climate 
variability, and a rapidly aging agricultural workforce, highlighting the urgent need for 
accessible, automated precision farming solutions  [2] [1] [4] [3]. Current agricultural 
technologies, however, are fragmented, costly, and fail to provide small -scale farmers 
with an integrated platform for real -time envir onmental monitoring and precise crop 
management. 
9 | P a g e  
 
First, farmers often manage separate irrigation , fertilization systems that rely on rigid, 
clock-based schedules, leading to inefficiencies and suboptimal crop care [8] [9]. Second, 
conventional automation lacks predictive capability and real time in-situ NPK awareness, 
exposing crops to stresses such as drought, waterlogging, chemical imbalances, and 
environmental pollution [17] [9] [18] [19]. Third, weed control remains a labor -intensive 
bottleneck often addressed by harmful blanket chemical herbicides [20]. While robotic 
alternatives exist, they suffer from critical integration gaps: some rely on rigid coordinate-
based systems that fail to detect weeds growing unexpectedly near crops  [16], while 
others depend on artificial crop markers that add operational complexity  [21]. 
Consequently, there is a lack of unified systems capable of real-time, chemical-free weed 
differentiation using natural feature recognition. 
To address these challenges, this study proposes an autonomous gantry-based precision 
farming system that integrates IoT multi-sensor fusion, computer vision for real-time plant 
phenotyping, and weather -adaptive control algorithms  [22] [23]. By combining mobility, 
irrigation, and automated nutrient management within a unified platform, the system aims 
to optimize water and fertilizer use, improve positioning accuracy, and enhance crop yield 
while reducing labor requirements compared to conventional manual methods.

1.5 Scope and Limitations 
This study focuses on the design, development, and evaluation of an IoT -enabled 
autonomous Cartesian gantry system intended for precision horticultural cultivation within 
a controlle d soil -based environment. The proposed system  integrates multi -modal 
sensing, intelligent decision -making, and robotic actuation into a unified cyber -physical 
agricultural platform capable of performing irrigation, NPK fertigation, monitoring, and 
mechanical weed mitigation. The system is designed to support the cultivation of rooted 
leafy crops, such as lettuce, kangkong, and spinach, wherein the gantry structure 
simultaneously serves as both a cultivation platform and a nursery environment for plant 
growth and maintenance. 
The scope includes the implementation of a fixed-frame XYZ Cartesian gantry capable of 
precise and repeatable movement for targeted soil intervention. Environmental and soil 
parameters, including soil moisture, nutrient concentration (NPK), pH, electrical 
conductivity, temperature, and humidity , are acquired using integrated sensing units. 
Weather-adaptive irrigation is implemented through the integration of  cloud-based 
meteorological data obtained from the OpenWeather API and localized rainfall detection 
using a raindrop sensor module. Short -term rainfall likelihood is inferred using forecast 
16 | P a g e  
 
parameters such as cloud cover percentage, relative humidity  and probability of 
precipitation. These forecast indicators are combined with real -time rainfall detection to 
modulate irrigation scheduling and prevent unnecessary water application prior to 
anticipated rainfall events.  Nutrient management is performed using machine learning –
assisted inference and fuzzy logic-based fertigation control, a llowing adaptive nutrient 
delivery based on real -time soil conditions. In addition, a computer vision subsystem 
utilizing a TinyML-based detection model is implemented to identify weeds and perform 
coordinate-based mechanical intervention through the gantr y’s end -effectors. System 
status, sensor readings, and operational controls are transmitted to an application-based 
user interface enabling live monitoring, data visualization, and remote supervisory control. 
The study evaluates system performance in terms  of sensing reliability, mechanical 
positioning accuracy, and computer vision weed detection capability under prototype -
level operating conditions. The overall goal is to demonstrate a unified autonomous 
farming platform capable of improving resource efficiency, reducing manual intervention, 
and supporting precision horticultural practices. 
Despite the capabilities of the proposed system, several limitations are acknowledged. 
The study is restricted to small-scale horticultural cultivation and does not address large-
scale agricultural deployment or open-field commercial farming environments. The gantry 
workspace dimensions limit the cultivation area and may not be directly scalable without 
structural redesign. The system is intended for rooted plants and doe s not address 
automated seed germination, transplantation, or seedling propagation processes beyond 
nursery-level maintenance. 
17 | P a g e  
 
The computer vision subsystem relies on publicly available image datasets for model 
training; therefore, detection performance ma y decrease under extreme lighting 
conditions, occlusions, unfamiliar weed species, or environmental variations not 
represented within the dataset. The accuracy of weather -adaptive irrigation depends on 
third-party weather API data, which may introduce fore casting uncertainties due to 
microclimate variations at the local site. The raindrop sensor module employed for 
reactive rainfall validation provides binary moisture detection rather than volumetric 
rainfall measurement, limiting the system’s ability to qu antify rainfall intensity compared 
to tipping-bucket rain gauges. 
The machine learning–assisted fertigation model is trained using external datasets and 
therefore provides advisory dosage estimation based on generalized patterns rather than 
laboratory-grade soil analysis. The fertigation system utilizes a standardized nutrient 
solution and does not perform independent real -time balancing of separate nitrogen, 
phosphorus, and potassium sources. Sensor accuracy may also be influenced by soil 
heterogeneity, se nsor drift, calibration inconsistencies, and environmental noise over 
prolonged operation. 
From a mechanical perspective, positioning accuracy depends on calibration of belts, 
lead screws, and stepper motors, and may be affected by vibration, mechanical wear, or 
outdoor environmental exposure. Power interruptions, wireless communication instability, 
and external environmental factors such as heavy rainfall or excessive heat may influence 
operational reliability. Finally, the system prioritizes proof -of-concept functionality and 
performance evaluation rather than industrial-level durability. 
18 | P a g e  
 
Despite these limitations, the study establishes a practical foundation for sensor -fused 
autonomous farming systems and provides a scalable framework for future improvements 
in precision agricultural robotics and intelligent cultivation platforms. 
1.6 Definition of Terms 
• Bounding Box – A rectangular spatial boundary predicted by the TinyML model 
that identifies the class (crop or weed) and location of an object within the im age 
grid. 
• Cartesian Gantry System – A fixed-frame XYZ mechanical robot architecture that 
moves the tool head over the cultivation bed with millimeter precision, eliminating 
the soil compaction caused by wheeled robots. 
• Centroid Extraction  – The geometric p rocess of calculating the center of a 
predicted bounding box (xc, yc) to provide a precise target coordinate for the 
mechanical weeding actuator. 
• Computer Vision – The image processing subsystem utilizing an ESP32 -CAM 
and a TinyML -based neural network to v isually detect and differentiate between 
crops and weeds in real-time. 
• Confidence Score  – A probability value (C) generated by the object detection 
model that represents the certainty that an object exists within a grid cell and 
matches the ground-truth bounding box. 
• Confusion Matrix – A performance evaluation tool used during the vision system 
validation to derive metrics such as Precision and Recall by comparing detection 
results against manually labeled ground-truth images. 
19 | P a g e  
 
• Defuzzification – The final stage of the fuzzy inference process where aggregated 
qualitative linguistic results are converted back into a precise numerical command, 
such as the exact number of seconds a pump should remain active. 
• End-Effector (All -in-One Tool Head)  – A consolidated ph ysical assembly 
mounted on the gantry’s Z-axis that integrates a pump nozzle, an NPK sensor slot, 
and a mechanical weeder into a single unit to avoid complex tool -changing 
mechanisms. 
• Euclidean Distance – A mathematical metric used during positioning accur acy 
tests to measure the spatial error between a target coordinate and the actual 
physical location of the tool head. 
• FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)  – The 3D printing method used to fabricate 
custom mechanical brackets, motor mounts, and the tool head ass embly using 
PETG material for rapid prototyping and cost reduction. 
• Fertigation – The automated, simultaneous delivery of water and dissolved NPK 
fertilizers to the soil bed based on real-time nutrient gap analysis. 
• Fuzzification – The initial computational step in fuzzy logic where precise, "crisp" 
sensor measurements are converted into qualitative linguistic sets like "Dry," 
"Optimal," or "Wet". 
• Fuzzy Logic – An adaptive control algorithm that translates varying environmental 
inputs, such as soil moisture  and nutrient levels, into precise pumping durations 
for water and fertilizer. 
• Gating – A supervisory control mechanism used to inhibit or allow an automated 
process based on specific priority data; in this study, it refers to forecast -based 
20 | P a g e  
 
gating, which suppresses scheduled irrigation if rain is predicted by the 
OpenWeather API. 
• INT8 Format – A quantized numerical format used to compress 32 -bit floating-
point weights of the neural network, allowing the weed detection model to run 
efficiently on memory-constrained microcontrollers. 
• Internet of Things (IoT)  – The networked digital architecture connecting the 
microcontrollers, physical sensors, cloud weather APIs, and the remote mobile 
dashboard. 
• IoU (Intersection over Union)  – A spatial evaluation metric tha t quantifies the 
accuracy of a predicted bounding box by measuring the overlap between the 
predicted region and the ground-truth region. 
• Kinematic Errors – Inaccuracies in the mechanical motion of a robot; Cartesian 
systems minimize these errors compared t o articulated arms, which suffer from 
non-linear positioning shifts at the edge of their reach. 
• Mamdani-type Fuzzy Inference  – A specific fuzzy logic framework used in the 
system to convert predictive recommendations and real -time soil conditions into 
deterministic actuator control signals. 
• Master-Slave Configuration  – A distributed control architecture where the 
"Master" (ESP3 2) handles high -level sensing and IoT connectivity, while the 
"Slave" (Arduino Uno) executes real-time motor and hydraulic actuation. 
• Nitrogen Use Efficiency (NUE)  – A resource efficiency metric calculated as the 
ratio of total fresh yield to the total vol ume of fertilizer used, aiming for a 50% 
increase through precision application. 
21 | P a g e  
 
• Pedological – Pertaining to soil science, specifically the study of soil’s physical 
and chemical properties and how they impact nutrient loading, assimilation, and 
land fertility. 
• Peristaltic Pump – A precision dosing motor that propels fluid by compressing a 
flexible tube, ensuring corrosive nutrients never contact internal mechanical parts 
while delivering exact volumes of water or fertilizer. 
• PETG (Polyethylene Terephthalate Glycol) – A UV-resistant and high-durability 
3D printing filament chosen for outdoor structural components to prevent warping 
and brittleness under direct sunlight. 
• Phenological – Relating to the study of cyclic and seasonal natural phenomena, 
such as specific growth stages of a crop, which the system classifies to determine 
resource needs. 
• Precision Horticulture – A cultivation approach focused on targeted, site-specific 
application of water and nutrients based on exact plant needs rather than wasteful 
"blanket" application. 
• Quantization – A TinyML optimization technique used to convert complex neural 
network weights into smaller formats (like INT8), allowing AI models to run on 
resource-constrained microcontrollers like the ESP32. 
• RMSE (Root Mean Square Error)  – A statistical measure used to evaluate the 
accuracy of the XGBoost regression model in predicting optimal fertilizer dosages 
against ground-truth values. 
22 | P a g e  
 
• Sensor Fusion  – The computational process of combining data from multiple 
distinct sources, such as physical soil sensors and cloud -based weather APIs, to 
guide the system's decision-making. 
• TinyML – An optimized machine learning framework designed to run inference 
locally on edge devices (like the ESP32-S3) without relying on expensive, power-
hungry external computers or cloud latency. 
• UART (Universal Asynchronous Receiver -Transmitter) - Hardware serial 
communication lines (TX/RX) used to asynchronously transmit G-code commands 
and synchronize status feedback between the central ESP32 (Master) and the 
secondary Arduino controller (Slave). 
• Water Use Efficiency (WUE) – An agronomic metric representing the ratio of total 
fresh yield produced to the total volume of water applied, aiming for a 60% increase 
through sensor-based scheduling. 
• Weather-Adaptive Control  – Predictive decision -making logic that cross -
references OpenWeather API forecasts and physical raindrop sensors to suppress 
scheduled irrigation if rain is imminent. 
• WebSocket Protocol (RFC 6455)  – A persistent, bidirectional communication 
standard used to transmit real -time coordinate commands and live event logs 
between the mobile dashboard and the gantry with sub-100ms latency. 
• XGBoost – A high -performance machine learning regression model integrated 
into the system to analyze real -time soil data and predict the optimal fertilizer 
dosage required for the crops

2.1.1 Global Resource Collapse 
With the global population projected to grow significantly by 2050, traditional agricultural 
methods are struggling to meet the rising demand for food as available farmland becomes 
increasingly unusable  [24]. Conventional soil-based farming often accelerates nutrient 
depletion, contributing to long -term soil exhaustion that limits future productivity. 
Continuing to depend on resource-intensive methods therefore presents  a measurable 
risk to global food security  and highlights the need for more efficient  and adaptive 
agricultural systems. 
Rapid population growth places additional pressure on already limited natural resources, 
while current agricultural practices intensify  environmental degradation through 
greenhouse gas emissions and pollution. Traditional food systems are increasingly 
vulnerable to climate variability, necessitating a shift toward innovations that reduce land 
and water  consumption. As emphasized in  [25], preventing a global crisis requires a 
systemic transformation that integrates modern biotechnology and artificial intelligence to 
produce food sustainably without exhausting the planet. 
Building on the urgent need for change, major global assessments warn that the essential 
systems of land, soil, and water are currently being pushed to a breaking point [2] reports 
that human activities are rapidly degrading the soil and depleting water supplies, creating 
a dangerous situation where the very resources needed to grow food are being 
exhausted. This deterioration is occurring just as the world faces the massive challenge 
of needing to produce nearly 50 percent more food by the year 2050 to feed a la rger

population. Consequently, avoiding a future crisis requires a significant shift in how we 
govern and manage these natural resources to ensure they remain productive for future 
generations.

degradation compromises food production, a crisis often concealed in wealthy nations by 
the intensive use of synthetic fertilizers that masks underlying soil deterioration. This 
challenge is compounded by extreme structural inequality, where the largest 0.1 percent

2.4 Logic and Weather Forecasting 
Having established the mechanical precision of the Cartesian gantry and the robust 
connectivity of the hybrid control architecture, the focus must shift to the system's 
cognitive layer. Physical actuation and data acquisition serve only as the mechanisms for 
intervention; true autonomous efficiency is determined effectively by the intelligence of 
the governing algorithms.  Merely reacting to immediate sensor thresholds is often 
insufficient for sustainable agriculture. Therefore, to prevent resource wastage and 
ensure crop survival, the robotic platform must transcend simple reactive protocols. This 
section analyzes the development of adaptive control logic, specifically the integration of 
predictive meteorological modeling to replace rigid, calendar -based schedules with 
dynamic, weather-aware decision-making. 
2.4.1 The Failures of "Dumb" Timers & Protocols 
Current agricultural automation often relies on rigid temporal logic or reactive thresholds, 
which fail to account for the dynamic biological needs of crops. For instance, the "Farming 
Bot" developed by Kautsar et al. [8] utilizes a CNC architecture governed by a Real Time 
Clock (RTC) module. This system is programmed with a static schedule, executing 
watering processes strictly at 08:00 and 14:00 regardless of actual environmental 
conditions. While this automates the labor, it lacks the intelligence  to adapt to daily 
weather variations. Similarly, Elaydi [42] proposed a SCADA -based system that, while 
more advanced than a simple timer, relies on rigid "If/Then" threshold logic. The system 
uses sensors to check if soil moisture is "LOW" and the tank is "FULL," triggering a valve 
only when these specific binary conditions are met. This represents a reactive approach 
rather than a predictive one. 
46 | P a g e  
 
The reliance on these rigid protocols mirrors the inefficiencies of manual irrig ation 
management, where growers must constantly calculate their soil's "bank balance" of 
water to avoid the "expense" of evapotranspiration. Drost and Maughan [59] note that 
improper management of this balance leads to signifi cant yield reduction or nutrient 
leaching. The biological consequences of these rigid or manual failures are severe. Orsák 
et al. [17] demonstrated that timing errors leading to drought stress result in the significant 
destruction of photosynthetic pigments, specifically reducing chlorophyll a and b contents 
in potato leaves by over 60%. Conversely, over -irrigation resulting from "dumb" timers 
causes waterlogging. Manghwar et al. [9] explain that t his creates hypoxic or anoxic 
conditions in the root zone, leading to an energy crisis, the rapid accumulation of Reactive 
Oxygen Species (ROS) like hydrogen peroxide, and ultimately cell death and plant 
senescence. 
2.4.2 Predictive Meteorological Modeling 
To mitigate the risks of static timing, recent research emphasizes the integration of real -
time weather data as a critical evolution in smart farming. Das and Nayak [60] 
characterize the integration of IoT and AI-powered local weather forecasting as a "Game-
Changer" for agriculture. They propose a framework that transcends simple sensing; it 
moves from a "Data Acquisition Layer" to a "Decision Making Layer" that utilizes machine 
learning (such as Random Forest regressors) to predict future environmental conditions. 
This allows the system to be predictive rather than merely reactive, optimizing resource 
allocation before stress occurs.

2.5 NPK: Precision and Calibration 
While hydric stability is a prerequisite for crop health, nutrient availability ultimately 
governs plant productivity. Over - or under -fertilization reduces yield, degrades soil 
structure, and increases environmental risk. In practical field  conditions, many farming 
systems rely on standardized fertilizer blends rather than variable nutrient formulation. 
Therefore, precision agriculture does not always require chemical customization, but 
instead demands accurate control over when, how often, and how much fertilizer is

automated delivery mechanisms relevant to gantry -based systems that employ

2.5.1 Pedological Impacts of Synthetic Nutrient Loading 
Chemical fertilizers, particularly Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium (NPK), serve as 
the fundamental engine of modern food security; however, their unchecked application 
has created a critical dependency that threatens the very land it seeks to cultivate. Recent 
data indicates tha t while these inputs drive yield, their indiscriminate use has skewed 
nutrient ratios to a precarious 11.8:4.6:1 in recent years, far exceeding optimal biological 
limits [75]. This "blanket application" strategy creates a paradox where the pursuit of 
immediate abundance triggers long -term soil acidification and groundwater 
contamination, signaling an urgent need to transiti on from volume -based farming to 
precision-based stewardship. 
Compounding this chemical imbalance is the economic desperation found  in arid and 
developing regions, where the imperative to maximize short-term harvests drives farmers 
to effectively "mine" the  soil. Intensive, manual fertilizer application often leads not to 
sustainable growth, but to the leaching of nitrates into groundwater and the eutrophication 
of surface water bodies [76]. This inefficiency creates a toxic legacy; as excess chemicals 
accumulate in the food chain as heavy metal residues, they pose significant public health 
risks, validating the argument that sustainable farming is not just an environmental 
preference, but a biological necessity for human safety. 
The damage extends beyond immediate pollution to the systemic degradation of the soil’s 
living ecosystem. While synthetic inputs facilitate agricultural intensification, their energy-
intensive production contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emission s, and their 
pervasive presence disrupts the soil microbial communities essential for natural fertility  
[77]. This disruption creates a cycle of dependency where the soil loses its natural 
56 | P a g e  
 
resilience, becoming increasingly acidic and lifeless. Consequently, the adoption of 
intelligent agricultural systems is not merely about automation, but about restoring the 
balance between productivity and ecosystem resilience. 
Despite these ecological hazards, the physiological necessity  of NPK cannot be 
dismissed, as these elements remain the "backbone" of plant life, driving  chlorophyll 
synthesis and ATP energy transfer [78]. The challenge, therefore, is not the elimination 
of inputs, but the precise calibra tion of their delivery. Experimental trials on Brassica 
oleracea (Cabbage) demonstrate that a 50:50 integration of organic and inorganic 
fertilizers achieved a peak yield of 25,664 kg ha ⁻¹, whereas exclusive inorganic 
application caused physiological stress and "leaf scorching" [79]. This confirms that the 
future of soil -based automation lies in a robotic system capable of delivering this 
balanced, site-specific nutrient regime to optimize yield without degrading the earth.  For 
small-scale robotic platforms, implementing full chemical nutrient blending is often 
economically impractical. Consequently, many autonomous systems such as the 
FarmBot [35] adopt calibrated dispensing of pre -mixed fertilizers, focusing on delivery 
accuracy rather than compositional variation. This approach preserves affordability while 
still reducing waste and environmental impact through controlled application.

2.6.1 Pathological and Economic Impacts of Weed Competition 
Weed c ompetition remains one of the most significant biological threats to crop 
productivity. In precision agriculture systems, weeds undermine site -specific resource 
allocation by diverting water and nitrogen , phosphorus, potassium (NPK) nutrients 
intended for cultivated plants. Rajalakshmi et al. [16] report that weed interference can 
result in an average production loss of 34% due to competition for sunlight, water, and 
essential minerals. This competition nullifies the benefits of sensor-driven nutrient dosing, 
as resources delivered with millimeter accuracy are absorbed by unintended species.

Beyond biological interference, weeds impose severe economic and labor burdens. 
Sethia et al. [20] emphasize that manual weeding is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and

than 1% of applied ch emicals contribute directly to weed control, while the remainder 
contaminates the surrounding environment and exposes growers to respiratory and 
dermatological health risks. Such inefficiencies contradict the sustainability goals of 
precision agriculture and highlight the need for targeted, automated alternatives. 
Labor shortages further exacerbate the problem. Rajalakshmi et al.  [16] argue that

high-density cultivation systems. Automated weed detection and removal therefore 
address both economic and logistical constraints by replacing repetitive labor with 
repeatable machine-driven precision. 
However, reliable real -time discrimination between crops and weeds remains a major 
technical challenge. Raja et al. [21] observed that conventional vision systems struggle

removal. These limitations establish the need for advanced sensing and machine learning 
frameworks capable of operating in complex agricultural scenes.

mandatory for gantry -based vision systems operating in realist ic, non -controlled 
environments. 
2.6.2.2 AI Models 
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have proven highly effective for automated 
feature extraction, surpassing manual observation in identifying complex plant traits [95]. 
Deep learnin g architectures consistently demonstrate high accuracy across diverse 
tasks; for instance, YOLOv4 by Rodrigues et al. achieved 87.0% in classifying 
phenological stages across multiple crops  [96], while pre -trained models like 
DenseNet121 by Garg et al. [83] and Faster R-CNN by Moraitis [13] reached 99.6% for 
crop disease detection and 92% for mature plant identification , respectively. 
Beyond basic classification, CNNs facilitate highly precise physical modeling. Buxbaum-
inspired architectures have achieved mass estimation errors as low as 1.45 grams [40], 
and lightweight models like YOLOv8n-seg can track vertical growth with an absolute error 
of only 7.39 mm [97]. These structural analyses are often complemented by quantitative 
69 | P a g e  
 
health assessments, such as the Excess Green Index (ExG), to measure vegetation vigor 
[98]. Real-time robotic execution is also well -documented in the literature, with YOLOv8 
achieving ultra-low inference speeds of 0.7 ms per image to guide motor encoders [99], 
and YOLOv3 localizing weeds at 45 frames per second using JSON spatial mapping [16]. 
While the aforementioned studies rely heavily on resource -intensive architecture like 
YOLO, these mainstream models often feature intricate networks and parameter counts 
that vastly surpass the capabilities of low -power hardware, making them impossible to 
run on the resource -constrained edge provided by microcontrollers like the ESP32. 
Consequently, the transition to Tiny Machine Learning (Tin yML) is necessary for 
achieving real-time, EDGE weeding. Song et al.  [100] highlight that mainstream deep 
learning models often feature intricate architectures that surpass the capabilities of field-
deployed hardware, arguing t hat even lightweight YOLO series models can exhibit 
unacceptable inference latency on edge devices. They emphasize that optimized 
techniques like quantization and pruning are required to facilitate the transition from 
laboratory settings to practical, resource-constrained agricultural applications. 
Building on the capabilities of such hardware, Mahajan et al. [101] demonstrate that 
integrating TinyML libraries directly onto an ESP32 camera module allows for real -time 
weed identification and classification without relying on expensive, power-hungry external 
computers. Their research establishes a holistic approach where these lightweight 
algorithms enable the system to "see" and differentiate between crops and weeds with 
exceptional accuracy during field trials. By fusing computer vision with proximity sensors, 
they optimized the efficiency and adaptability of autonomous weeding systems for 
sustainable agriculture. 
70 | P a g e  
 
Finally, Donskoy et al. [102] validate that microcontroller-tier hardware is not only viable 
but optimal when energy efficiency and hardware costs are the primary constraints for 
field monitoring. Their research shows that classification accuracies exceeding 87% can 
be achieved on the ESP32 us ing TinyML, significantly reducing power consumption to 
approximately 1 W compared to 5 –15 W for higher -end single -board computers. This 
confirms that implementing CNNs on low -power microcontrollers enables effective plant 
object classification while drast ically reducing the energy footprint and cost of field 
monitoring systems 
2.6.3 Vision-Guided Weeding & Mechanical Intervention 
2.6.3.1 Precision Imaging & Cartesian Motion Control 
Building on the architectural analysis in Section 2.1, the choice of a Cartesian gantry 
provides the mechanical rigidity required for high -precision weeding. As established by 
Moraitis et al. [13] and Subrata & Melania [13], this fixed coordinate system minimizes 
vibration and eliminates the complex calibration needed for articulated arms, achieving 
positioning errors as low as 0.4 mm. This stability is a prerequisite for the proposed 
system, ensuring that the computer vision module can capture consistent imagery and 
that the mechanical end-effector can target individual weeds without disturbing adjacent 
crops. 
Advanced control logic allows these machines to interact with the environment effectively 
through coordinate mapping. Muriki et al. [37] utilized photogrammetry to generate point 
clouds for pose estimation. Their system achieved a mean orientation error of only 7.7 
degrees, providing the spatial awareness required for delicate contact-based interactions. 
This is backed by Das et al. [23], who implemented a CoreXY mechanism controlled by 
71 | P a g e  
 
inverse kinematics to achieve a minimum positioning error of less than 0.4 mm. Finally, 
Zhai and Zhang [49] verified that XYZ-type robots with additional degrees of freedom are 
the most effective for complex surface interaction while maintaining high stiffness. 
2.6.3.2 Mechanical and Chemical Weeding Methods 
Modern agricultural interventions are undergoing a significant transition from the uniform 
application of agrochemicals toward site-specific delivery systems designed to enhance 
efficiency and safety. Early developments in this field often focused on wheeled platforms, 
such as those discussed by Adamides et al.  [103]. Their research introduced a semi -
autonomous mobile robotic sprayer for vineyards that utilized teleoperation to allow 
growers to manage spraying tasks from a safe distance. While effective for keeping 
farmers away from the immediate vicinity of chemical dispersion, these wheeled systems 
often face challenges related to peripheral vision and the complexity of navigating 
unstructured field environments. Complementary efforts in precision agriculture have also 
utilized Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), as noted by Garg et al. [83], who emphasized 
the use of vision-based UAV systems to isolate crops from weeds. These aerial platforms 
target a reduction in the broad spread of herbicides to safeguard crop quality, though they 
remain distinct from ground-based precision tools. 
Das et al. [60] developed a gantry sprayer capable of hitting 73.3% of targets within a 
tight statistical margin, demonstrating the feasibility of site -specific fluid delivery. Raja et 
al. [21] reported 98.93% classification accuracy in a micro -jet herbicide system, 
establishing a benchmark for precision spraying. Despite these advances, chemical 
reliance remains problematic. Sethia et al.  [20] and Das et al. both note that agrochemical 
72 | P a g e  
 
overuse contributes to environmental contamination, rising production costs, and health 
hazards. 
Consequently, recent research prioritizes mechanical weed removal. Sethia et al. [20] 
introduced a rotating blade end -effector achieving 99.5% scraping accuracy, allowing 
removed biomass to decompose naturally as fertilizer. Rajalakshmi et al. implemented a 
plunge weeder that physically suppresses weeds without chemicals, demonstrating a 
sustainable alternative aligned with Agriculture 4.0 goals. Mechanical intervention 
eliminates chemical residues while preserving soil health, making it the preferred strategy 
for environmentally responsible automation. 
2.7 Crop & Soil Science 
While the preceding sections establish the mechanical and computational architecture of 
the platform, the operational parameters of the system are ultimately dictated by the 
physiological requirements of the target cultivars. Precision agriculture is not an end, but 
a mechanism to maintain biological subjects within their optimal metabolic windows. To 
validate the efficacy of the gantry -based system, it is necessary to establish the specific 
moisture thresholds, nutrient assimilation rates, and stress-response benchmarks for the 
crops under cultivation. The following discourse evaluates the pedological and 
physiological characteristics of the selected test subjects , Spinach, Kangkong, Lettuce, 
and Cucumber, to define the set-points for the system's autonomous control logic. 
2.7.1 Stress Testing Subjects 
Spinach generally exhibits low nitrogen uptake efficiency, often requiring substantial 
fertilization to achieve market -standard leaf quality. However, the application of 
biostimulants such as legume-derived protein hydrolysates has been shown to increase 
73 | P a g e  
 
nitrogen uptake efficiency by up to 50%, even when fertilizer inputs are reduced [104]. 
This indicates that optimizing nutrient assimilation through precision treatments can 
sustain crop performance while minimizing nitrogen surplus in soil -based systems.  
Automated sensor-based irrigation scheduling allows for precise moisture control, with 
experiments showing that maintaining soil moisture at 75% of Available Soil Moisture 
(ASM) significantly improves spin ach yield compared to traditional surface methods. 
Additionally, this regime increases Water Use Efficiency (WUE) to 3.15 kg m ⁻³ and 
reduces water consumption by approximately 60%, verifying the economic and 
environmental viability of precision irrigation systems in controlled environments [105]. 
Research utilizing drip irrigation for Ipomoea aquatica (Kangkong) indicates a low daily 
water consumption requirement, averaging approximately 4.40 mm per day. Over a 
standard 25 -day harvesting period, a cumulative water application of 108.5 mm was 
sufficient to sustain optimal growth, achieving an average plant height of 334 mm and a 
yield density of 328.67 g m ⁻² [106]. Optimizing growing media through the addition of 
organic amendments such as chicken manure and vermicast has  been shown to 
significantly elevate soil nutrient levels, including total nitrogen and exchangeable 
potassium, which are critical for intensive soil -based systems. Empirical results indicate 
that optimal mixture ratios are species-specific; for instance, Ipomoea aquatica achieved 
maximum biomass in a 50:50 mix of chicken manure and vermicast, whereas Brassica 
rapa performed best in a composite mixture containing 25% garden soil [107]. 
Optimizing the temporal delivery of nutrien t solutions is essential for sustainable 
vegetable production, as water availability directly influences crop physiology and 
biomass accumulation. Experimental trials on Lactuca sativa  (Lettuce) have 
74 | P a g e  
 
demonstrated that a daily morning irrigation schedule at 0900 hours produces significantly 
superior vegetative growth compared to alternative-day or two-day interval regimes [108]. 
Specifically, plants subjected to daily irrigation exhibited the highest values for 
morphological parameters, including plant height, leaf number, and total fresh weight, 
indicating that consistent moisture availability is critical for preventing water stress . 
Beyond physical dimensions, physiological performance metrics such as Net Assimilation 
Rate (NAR) and Relative Growth Rate (RGR) were maximized under the daily regime, 
suggesting that frequent irrigation supports higher metabolic activity and photosynthetic 
efficiency. Conversely, reducing irrigation frequency resulted in significant declines in leaf 
area and biomass, confirming that specific irrigation timing is a limiting factor for achieving 
market-standard yields in controlled environment agriculture. 
Experimental analysis of Plantago maritima (Sea Plantain) demonstrates that while the 
species is highly resilient, optimal biomass accumulation is strictly confined to a soil 
moisture range of 50% to 75%. Deviations from this window, including low moisture 
conditions (25%) and waterlogging, result in significant suppression of both vegetative 
growth and reproductive output, particularly reducing the number of flower stalks [109]. 
Furthermore, photosynthetic metrics such as the Performance Index Total have been 
identified as sensitive indicators of moisture stress, often signaling physiological decline 
before morphological symptoms become visible.  Cucumber (Cucumis sativus L.) 
production is highly sensitive to soil moisture deficits, with studies indicating that 
maintaining soil water levels below 60% of pot capacity significantly hampers vegetative 
growth, including vine length and leaf area. Furthermore, water stress adversely affects 
reproductive performance by increasing the ratio of male to female flowers and elevating 
75 | P a g e  
 
rates of flower abortion, which directly limits fruit set. To optimize yield and fruit quality in 
controlled soil-based systems, it is recommended to maintain soil moisture at a minimum 
of 80% of field capacity throughout the productive phase [110]. 
2.7.2 Protocols & Monitoring Benchmarks 
Internal physiological responses to water deficit, such as the rapid accumulation of 
Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) and hormonal signal transduction, actively degrade 
cellular structures through lipid peroxidation and protein denaturation well before 
morphological signs of wilting appear [9]. This latent phase of stress represents a critical 
window where metabolic damage accumulates despite the crop retaining a healthy visual 
appearance, rendering traditional manual observation ineffective for preventing early -
stage yield loss. Therefore, deploying precision sensors to detect these underlying 
biochemical changes is necessary to trigger irrigation interventions before the plant 
reaches the threshold of irreversible physical deterioration. 
Quantitative assessments of lettuce production indicate that conventional soil -based 
methods are far less energy -intensive than controlled environment systems, requiring 
approximately 82 times less energy per kilogram of produce due to the absence of 
artificial lighting and climate control  [44]. However, traditional soil cultivation faces 
significant challenges in water use efficiency, consuming roughly 250 L kg⁻¹ compared to 
just 20 L kg ⁻¹ in hydroponic setups, a disparity that precision irrigation systems aim to 
address. 
Physiological disorders such as tip burn, characterized by necrotic breakdown or brown 
spots along the margins of young leaves, serve as a primary visual indicator of moisture 
stress in leafy green production. Although  this condition physically manifests as a 
76 | P a g e  
 
localized calcium deficiency , it is fundamentally caused by irregular evapotranspiration 
and interrupted water transport rather than insufficient soil nutrient levels [59]. Therefore, 
the detection of these specific marginal defects provides a robust optical proxy for 
identifying irrigation system failures, allowing for corrective intervention before the crop 
suffers irrever sible yield loss. By leveraging high -resolution imaging to monitor these 
stress symptoms, automated systems can ensure the consistent moisture supply 
necessary to prevent physiological disorders while avoiding the disease risks associated 
with over-irrigation. Lettuce exhibits an optimal morphological structure for depth -based 
monitoring, as its open rosette geometry presents a distinct canopy surface that 
minimizes self-occlusion relative to the cultivation substrate. This structural characteristic 
facilitates the deployment of lightweight instance segmentation models, which have been 
shown to achieve height measurement accuracies exceeding 94% using low -cost RGB-
D sensors  [97]. Consequently, the crop’s physical properties signi ficantly reduce the 
computational complexity required for background separation and surface plane 
detection in automated gantry systems. 
Contrary to traditional guidelines emphasizing early morning watering, research indicates 
that shifting irrigation events to afternoon hours (12:00–18:00) can significantly enhance 
physiological performance by mitigating diurnal heat load [111]. Specifically, plants 
subjected to this PM regime exhibited an 86% increase in net Carbon Dioxide assimilation 
rates during late afternoon and a reduction in substrate temperature of 2–3°C compared 
to those irrigated at predawn. These findings suggest that programmable gantry systems 
should leverage afternoon cooling cycles to mitigate thermal stress and maximize water-
use efficiency rather than relying solely on morning schedules.

3.1.2 System Overview 
The proposed system is an IoT-enabled autonomous Cartesian Gantry platform designed 
for precision soil cultivation through the integration of sensing, intelligent decision-making, 
and robotic actuation. The system combines multi -modal environmental sensing, 
weather-adaptive irrigation, machine learning –assisted NPK f ertigation, and computer 
vision–guided mechanical weeding into a unified cyber -physical agricultural framework. 
Soil parameters such as moisture, nutrient concentration, pH, electrical conductivity, 
temperature, and humidity are continuously acquired using  a 7 -in-1 soil sensor and 
supplementary sensing modules, while atmospheric information is retrieved through 
cloud-based weather forecasting services. Captured data are processed by a distributed 
control architecture employing ESP32 and Arduino microcontrol lers, where high -level 
decision logic executes sensor fusion, predictive analysis, and control command 
generation. 
The robotic subsystem utilizes a fixed-frame XYZ Cartesian gantry capable of precise and 
repeatable movement for targeted irrigation, nutrient delivery, and mechanical weed 
mitigation. Computer vision processing using a TinyML-based detection pipeline enables 
identification of weeds from real-time camera input, allowing spatial coordinate mapping 
for accurate physical intervention. A fuzzy logic –based control layer regulates irrigation 
and fertigation operations based on environmental conditions and machine  learning 
predictions, ensuring demand-driven resource utilization rather than rigid schedule-based 
operation. All processed data and system statuses are transmitted to an application -

based user interface, enabling live monitoring, real -time visualization,  and remote 
supervisory control. Figure below presents the overall system architecture and 
operational flow of the proposed AGRI-3D platform.

The proposed system utilizes a fixed -frame Cartesian (XYZ) coordinate robot 
architecture. This configurati on was selected for its superior kinematic stability and 
simplified spatial mapping compared to polar or articulated arm configurations. Designed 
for outdoor agricultural environments, the structure prioritizes corrosion resistance, 
structural rigidity, and modular scalability. 
The primary chassis is constructed using 2020, 2040, and 2060 V -Slot Aluminum 
Extrusions, selected for their high rigidity -to-weight ratio and modularity.  This robust 
framework establishes a maximum physical travel distance of 910 x 910 x 550 mm across 
the X, Y, and Z axes. However, accounting for the physical boundaries of the gantry 
carriage, wheels, and the multi -tool end -effector, the system yields an actual, usable 
workable area of 850 x 850 mm. Defining this specific workable ar ea clearly establishes 
the physical spatial limitations of the tool head's reach within the cultivation bed.  
To facilitate rapid iteration and cost efficiency, all non-standard housings and specialized 
carriage parts were fabricated via Fused Deposition M odeling (FDM) using the 
Snapmaker Artisan 3 -in-1 printer at the CTU Main Computer Engineering Department. 
The Artisan’s 400x400x400 mm build volume enabled the production of large unibody 
parts, which minimizes mechanical failure modes by reducing the numb er of assembly 
points. For these components, PETG was chosen over standard PLA due to its 80°C 
glass transition temperature and superior UV resistance, ensuring the gantry remains 
structurally sound and resistant to warping when exposed to direct sunlight and outdoor 
heat.

3.2.4.6 Fluid Management System 
The Fluid Management System of the AGRI -3D platform serves as the central hydraulic 
infrastructure, supporting the robotic end-effector through a centralized fluid reservoir and 
pump array. Rather than relying on complex, multi -tank chemical blending mecha nisms 
commonly found in industrial setups, this system employs a calibrated dispensing 
strategy to deliver water and a standardized NPK nutrient solution. Governed by a 2 -
channel relay that independently toggles the water and fertilizer pumps, the hydrauli c 
output is routed directly to a dual-exit dispensing system located adjacent to the NPK 
sensor on the gantry's tool head. This ensures that exact volumes of water and dissolved 
fertilizers are delivered precisely to the root zone coordinates where soil deficiencies have 
been detected. 
 
Figure 3.30 Fertilizer Tank with Peristaltic Pump 

115 | P a g e  
 
Mounting the self-priming 12V DC peristaltic pump above the fertilizer reservoir efficiently 
draws liquid upward without requ iring a gravity -fed setup. By propelling fluid through 
continuous tube compression, the design ensures that corrosive nutrients remain 
completely isolated within the tubing, which prevents internal hardware degradation and 
cross-contamination. The extracted fluid is then routed through an independent line to an 
L-shaped dispensing hose integrated into the "All -in-One" end-effector adjacent to the 
NPK sensor, allowing the synchronized Cartesian gantry to accurately deliver calibrated 
nutrient doses directly to specific root zone coordinates where plant deficiencies are 
detected. 
 
Figure 3.31 Peristaltic Pump Housing 
The peristaltic pump functions as a precision dosing mechanism driven by a self-priming 
12V DC motor, propelling liquid through the physical compression of a flexible tube to 
deliver exact volumetric doses while completely isolating internal mechanical components 
from corrosive nutrients. To ensure resilience in the AGRI-3D platform's dynamic outdoor 
tropical environment, the pump and its sensitive electrical connections are enclosed 
within a specialized weatherproof housing to prevent hardware degradation. By 
configuring this enclosure to expose only the continuous flexible hoses while concealing 

116 | P a g e  
 
all vulnerable connections, the system establishes a highly secure physical barrier. This 
strategic design effectively shields the electromechanical components from external 
environmental threats, such as soil moisture, rain, and mud splashes, while the isol ated 
tubing simultaneously prevents internal motor degradation from the corrosive chemicals 
flowing through the system. 
 
Figure 3.32 Water Tank and BLDC Submersible Pump 
For the primary irrigation subsystem, the water reservoir utilizes a modified water jug 
where the standard dispensing faucet has been explicitly replaced by a Brushless DC 
(BLDC) submersible pump. Positioning this pump at the lowest point of the tank is a 
mechanical necessity, ensuring that the unit remains continuously submerged or directly 
supplied by the gravity-fed water flow required for optimal operation. Governed by a relay 
module and the platform's weather-adaptive fuzzy logic controller, this submersible pump 
efficiently extracts water from the jug and propels it through the centralized hydraulic 
routing to the straight dispensing tube integrated into the end -effector carriage. This 
continuous, submerged configuration guarantees that precise, demand -driven irrigation 
is delivered accurately to the targeted root zone coordinates without the risk of air 
introduction or pump cavitation. 

117 | P a g e  
 
 
Figure 3.33 Dual-Exit Dispensing System and Fluid Isolation 
To ensure strict fluid isolation and highly targeted application, the AGRI -3D system 
employs a dual-exit fluid routing architecture on the end-effector rather than a merged T-
junction. This design features a straight tube dedicated to primary freshwater irrig ation 
and an L-shaped hose dedicated exclusively to fertigation. 
By keeping the hydraulic lines completely physically separated from the reservoirs to the 
dispensing points, the system inherently eliminates the risk of cross -contamination or 
reverse siphon ing between the corrosive fertilizer solution and the freshwater supply. 
Furthermore, the L -shaped fertilizer hose is specifically engineered to dispense water -
soluble nutrient solutions (such as Peters 20 -20-20) at an angle directly onto the soil 
surface. This targeted trajectory ensures immediate root zone absorption while 
intentionally bypassing the plant foliage, thereby mitigating the risk of phytotoxicity or 
foliar burn. The straight tube manages standard vertical watering, allowing both the 
irrigation and fertigation subsystems to operate independently with maximum agronomic 
efficacy and hardware reliability.

3.2.4.7 Central Control and Processing Unit 
The system's intelligence is distributed across a dual-microcontroller architecture, utilizing 
a "Master -Slave" configuration to segregate high -level decision -making from real -time 
electromechanical actuation. This division ensures that computationally intensive tasks, 
such as wireless communication and sensor data aggregation, do not interfere with the 
precise timing required for stepper motor control. 
The ESP32 Development Board  serves as the central processing unit (Master). It is 
responsible for the system's cognitive layer, including: 
• Wireless Connectivity: It maintains a continuous Wi -Fi connection to the l ocal 
network, enabling bi -directional communication with the cloud dashboard (for 
remote monitoring) and the OpenWeather API (for predictive rainfall data). 
• Sensor Fusion: The ESP32 aggregates data from the 7 -in-1 NPK sensor (via 
RS485-to-TTL) and the capa citive moisture sensors. It executes the onboard 
Fuzzy Logic algorithm to determine the precise irrigation and fertigation 
requirements based on these inputs. 
• Command Generation ( UART TX ): Once a decision is made (e.g., "Initiate 
Watering Cycle"), the ESP3 2 Master constructs a standard G -code coordinate 
string and transmits it to the secondary controller via the hardware UART Transmit 
(TX) line. 
The Arduino NANO functions as the dedicated real -time actuator controller (Slave). It is 
tasked with the physical execution of the gantry's movements and hydraulic operations: 
119 | P a g e  
 
• Motor Interpretation: The Nano receives coordinate commands (G -code or 
step/direction signals) from the ESP32 and converts them into precise pulse trains 
for the stepper motor drivers (TMC2209). This ensures smooth acceleration and 
deceleration profiles for the X, Y, and Z axes without the jitter often caused by Wi-
Fi interrupts on single-chip systems. 
• Hydraulic Actuation: It directly manages the 5V relay modules controlling the DC 
water pumps and solenoid valves. By isolating these high-inductive loads from the 
sensitive ESP32, the Nano protects the primary processor from voltage spikes and 
electromagnetic interference (EMI). 
• Status Feedback and Safety ( UART RX): The Nano utilizes its UART Transmit 
(TX) line to provide the ESP32's Receive (RX) line with real -time feedback. It 
monitors hardware limit switches for the Z -axis and the TMC2209 StallGuard for 
end-stop detection on the gantry frame. If a physical limit is triggered or a stall is 
detected, the Nano halts motor power immediately and reports the collision status 
flag back to the Master via the UART serial line. 
3.2.4.8 Arduino Slave Firmware and State Management 
While the Master-Slave architecture isolates the ESP32 from electrical noise, the Arduino 
Nano requires strictly defined operational states to prevent power surges on the 12V 
SMPS and ensure positional accuracy. To achieve this, the Nano operates on GRBL -
Agri3d, a customized fork of the open -source GRBL firmware specifically modified to 
accommodate the gantry's unique hardware pin routing and hardware UART 
communication. This custom  firmware enforces a distinct separation between spatial 
translation and end-effector actuation. 
120 | P a g e  
 
As illustrated in the system's flow chart , the operation manages both concurrent and 
sequential tasks:  
Concurrent Motion State: The spatial translation of the Cartesian gantry requires strictly 
concurrent operation. Upon receiving coordinate targets from the ESP32, the Nano 
executes step pulses for the X, Y, and Z axes simultaneously to achieve linear 
interpolation. The Z axis will be purposely retracted during  movement to avoid crashing 
the end-effector to the growing plants.  
Sequential Tool Actuation State:  The interaction with the end -effector modules, such 
as the DC water pumps or the JGA25 -370 weeder motor managed by the 5V relays, is 
strictly sequential. The Nano ensures all axes broadcast a "Target Reached" flag and halt 
entirely before triggering the relay coils. Once the agricultural task completes, the system 
returns to an Idle state, awaiting the next command via the UART RX line.

3.2.4.9 Power Supply Management 
 
Figure 3.34 Power Supply Housing 
The system utilizes a modular power architecture designed to isolate high -voltage AC-
DC conversion from the logic and control circuitry. The primary power source is a 12V 
20A Switched-Mode Power Supply (SMPS), providing a total power capacity of 240W. 
This capacity was selected to accommodate the simultaneous peak inrush current of four 
NEMA 17 stepper motors, two pumps for fertigation, and auxiliary DC motor for weeding. 
To support the heterogeneous voltage requirements of the Farmbot's control system, the 
module implements a secondary DC -DC regulation stage using XL4005  Buck 
Converters:  
• 5V Logic Rail: Dedicated to powering the Arduino Nano and primary sensor sui te 
to ensure stable operation during motor switching. 

122 | P a g e  
 
• 3.3V Logic Rail: Dedicated to the ESP32 microcontroller to prevent brownouts 
during high-current Wi-Fi transmissions and to maintain signal integrity for wireless 
telemetry. 
To ensure long -term reliabil ity in an outdoor agricultural environment, the housing 
features an active push-pull cooling system: 
• Intake: A 6010 brushless DC fan is positioned to pull ambient air directly across 
the voltage regulators and the SMPS transformer. 
• Exhaust: A secondary 601 0 fan facilitates the rapid expulsion of thermal energy, 
creating a high -velocity airflow path that prevents heat saturation within the 3D -
printed enclosure. 
• Enclosure: The enclosure was designed to be fabricated via Additive 
Manufacturing. The material to be used for the enclosure is PETG.

perform a Hot Start and resume operations accurately after a power failure or unexpected 
reboot.

3.2.6.1 Weather-Adaptive Decision Algorithm 
The evolution of automated irrigation has transitioned from rigid scheduling to reactive 
sensing, with the current frontier being predictive weather adaptation. Traditional studies 
[8] adapted only "dumb timers" to trigger irrigation at fixed intervals, a method that lacks 
138 | P a g e  
 
the flexibility to adapt to atmospheric shifts. While reactive systems [83] water only when 
soil moisture falls below a threshold , the y remain vulnerable to over -saturation if 
precipitation occurs immediately after a cycle. 
Building upon these developments, the proposed Weather -Adaptive Decision Algorithm 
represents the next progression in irrigation intelligence. It functions as a prima ry 
supervisory gating mechanism for irrigation actuation, prioritizing atmospheric forecast 
data over instantaneous soil-triggered activation. Rather than responding solely to current 
soil conditions, the algorithm adopts a predictive -control strategy that  evaluates the 
probability of imminent precipitation before permitting irrigation. By allowing forecasted 
weather conditions to influence actuation decisions, the system reduces unnecessary 
water application, minimizes nutrient leaching, and preserves soil  structure. In this way, 
irrigation control evolves from simple automation to anticipatory, context-aware decision-
making aligned with precision agriculture principles.

3.4 Evaluation of System Performance 
The evaluation phase assesses the performance, reliability, and operational accuracy of 
the proposed AGRI-3D system. This phase focuses on validating the consistency of 
sensing units, the mechanical positioning precision of the gantry, and the effectivene ss 
of the computer vision –based weed detection subsystem. Performance assessment is 
conducted through controlled experimental trials to ensure that each subsystem meets 
the required operational standards for precision agricultural applications. The evaluat ion 
procedures are divided into three primary categories: sensor consistency and functionality 
testing, mechanical positioning accuracy testing, and weed detection accuracy 
assessment.

The evaluation will focus on precision (repeatability) and response time. This validates 
that the sensors provide stable, non -random readings suitable for trend analysis and 
automated logic, a method supported by recent studies on low -cost electronic sensors.

Control System in regulating nutrient delivery. Since the system utilizes Binary Control

measuring the Actuation Duration and Dosing Volume required to restore soil parameters 
to optimal levels.

3.4.4 Weather-Adaptive Logic Validation 
3.4.4.1 API-Triggered Operational Suppression Test 
To validate the system’s ability to replace rigid schedules with weather -adaptive

will be set to trigger irrigation (e.g., < 30%). A "Rain" condition (Precipitation Probability > 
80%) will be simulated via the OpenWeather API. The system is considered successful if 
the ESP32 logs a "Watering Suppressed" event despite the low moisture reading. This 
confirms the API's role as a primary control override.

3.4.6 Comparative Agronomic Analysis 
This evaluation measures the biological impact of the Weather -Adaptive Sensor-Fusion

separate control plot (manual cultivation) throughout the study , which would require 
additional time, space, and labor, the comparison will be conducted using market-sourced 
mature plants of the same crop type obtained from local vendors. These plants serve as 
a real-world reference for typical farming outcomes under conventional practices. 
To ensure a fa ir comparison, the purchased samples will be selected using consistent 
criteria (same crop variety, maturity stage, and no visible disease or pest damage). Both 
Agri–3D-grown crops and market reference crops will be evaluated using standardized, 
non-destructive agronomic indicators, including leaf count, plant height, canopy 
diameter/spread, leaf length and width (or estimated leaf area), and fresh biomass

(market samples only or both if harvesting is allowed ). The analysis will report average 
values and va riability across multiple samples to capture natural differences among 
plants. 
This approach maintains the objective of assessing whether Agri –3D supports healthy 
vegetative development while avoiding the operational burden of running a parallel 
manual con trol group, and it provides a realistic comparison against crops produced 
under common local growing conditions.
